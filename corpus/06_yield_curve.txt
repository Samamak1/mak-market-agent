The Yield Curve as a Signal
The yield curve plots government bond yields across maturities, from short bills to long bonds. Normally it slopes upward because investors demand extra yield for locking money up longer. When short yields rise above long yields, the curve inverts, historically one of the more reliable recession warnings, because it implies tight policy today and expected rate cuts tomorrow. The curve's shape also drives bank profitability, since banks borrow short and lend long, so a flat or inverted curve compresses lending margins. Steepening and flattening moves are traded directly through bond futures and swaps, and equity investors track them as a read on growth expectations.
