IPO Lockup Expirations
After an initial public offering, insiders and early investors are typically restricted from selling for a set period, commonly around 180 days. When that lockup expires, a large block of shares becomes eligible for sale at once, and the anticipation alone can pressure the stock into the date. Actual outcomes vary: heavily shorted names sometimes rally on expiration as feared selling fails to appear and shorts cover. The float, the portion of shares actually available for trading, can multiply overnight, changing how the stock trades, its volatility, and its borrow market. Lockup calendars are public, making this one of the more schedulable supply events.
