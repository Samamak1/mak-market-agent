The VIX and Implied Volatility
The VIX measures the market's expectation of S&P 500 volatility over the next thirty days, derived from index option prices. It tends to spike when stocks fall quickly because demand for protective puts drives option premiums higher, which is why it is nicknamed the fear gauge. A low VIX signals calm but can also signal complacency, and volatility tends to cluster: quiet periods follow quiet periods until a shock arrives. Implied volatility is the option market's forecast, while realized volatility is what actually happens, and the gap between them is the edge volatility sellers try to harvest. Traders also watch the VIX futures curve, where inversion often marks acute stress.
