How Interest Rates Move Markets
Interest rates set the price of money, so when central banks raise or lower their policy rate, every asset gets repriced. Higher rates make bonds more attractive relative to stocks and raise the discount rate applied to future corporate earnings, which pressures equity valuations, especially for growth companies whose profits sit far in the future. Lower rates do the opposite, pushing investors out along the risk curve in search of return. Markets react less to the rate decision itself and more to the surprise relative to expectations already priced in. Forward guidance, the central bank's language about future policy, can move markets as much as the decision.
